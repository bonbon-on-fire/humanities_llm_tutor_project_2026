Course Structure:
This course is organized into three 4-week modules, each containing a series of video learning units with topics presented by subject experts. At the end of each module, students in the certificate track complete a comprehensive assignment on their case study city. These assignments support an investigation of Climate Action Plans (CAPs), with the potential to actively support and contribute to a UCCRN Regional Hub's climate action strategy.

Required text:
Rosenzweig, C., Solecki, W. D., Romero-Lankao, P., Mehrotra, S., Dhakal, S., & Ibrahim, S. A. (Eds.). (2018). Climate change and cities: Second assessment report of the urban climate change research network. Cambridge University Press. (Abbreviated ARC3.2)

Learning Objectives:
- Gain a deeper understanding of fundamental theories of governance and planning impacting urban climate mitigation and adaptation.
- Comprehend the relationships between multi-level governance and multi-level climate risk assessment.
- Improve understanding of the operation of actors and systems involved in structuring policy and urban planning in response to climate change.
- Articulate and advocate for the best practices in climate mitigation and adaptation planning in local urban contexts.

Course Assessment:
Weekly engagement activities include forum discussions, comprehension questions, and short quizzes. Certificate track students complete case study research assignments at the end of each module, assessed through peer review or summarized instructor feedback. Weekly time commitment: 4-6 hours per week.

AskTIM Tutor:
AskTIM is a problem-solving socratic AI tutor for the MIT Learn online learning platform. AskTIM's purpose is to help a learner work through a given exercise from their course. Learners can start texting AskTIM from an exercise page on MIT Learn by clicking on the corresponding button on. AskTIM's interface lives on a different platform integrated to MIT Learn through an iframe. The context of the exercise is already known to AskTIM and the interaction remains in the scope of this exercise. The learner is expected to do the work and present their solution attempts to the tutor. The mode of interaction is different from regular AI chatbots, that act as assistants. AskTIM can lead the interaction and ask the learner to performs tasks rather than the other way around.
AskTIM has been developed in a way that benefits the learning process and does not remove all effort from the learner to avoid reliance. We encourage using AskTIM rather than other available chatbots to ensure long-term learning, even if using askTIM is more demanding.
